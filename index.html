01

Learning outcome

The promise — what they'll know, understand, or be able to do. Broad, durable, never measured directly.

One sentence, per outcome — not per course. Most courses carry four to eight course-level outcomes across the whole term; this tour walks one chain under a single outcome at a time. It survives the final exam — it's what's left six months later. You never measure an outcome directly; you measure the objectives that add up to it.

Bloom's original three domains (1956) — most outcomes live mainly in one.

  • cognitive — can judge which approach fits an unfamiliar situation
  • psychomotor — can produce the thing to a standard the field would accept
  • affective — values it enough to choose it voluntarily, and is willing to change position when the evidence changes

"Learners make defensible decisions with incomplete information, and can explain their reasoning to someone who disagrees."

No verb here is measurable on its own — that's correct. An outcome is a destination, not a test item. Band 02 is where it becomes measurable.

Quick check

Point to the phrase in this sentence that's ready to measure right now, with no further work.

Trick question — none of it is measurable yet. That's exactly what makes this an outcome instead of an objective: it names a destination, not something you can check off yet. Band 02 is where pieces of it turn into something you can actually measure.

01 · Learning outcome — now write yours

Your turn

Six months after the course ends, what can this student do that they could not do on day one? Write it as one sentence with a person in it. Just one of your outcomes for now — pick the one you most want to build out.

Seam test 1

Could this outcome stand as one of just a handful of broad headings for your whole course — or does it already read like a single, narrow task?

Then it's really too specific to be a learning outcome yet. A learning outcome should be one of a small number of broad headings for the whole course (Harden, 2002) — most courses work well with four to eight — broad enough to hold weeks of detailed work underneath it, even before that detail is written.

02

Learning objective

The contract — one observable move, unit or week scale. The only band you can measure directly.

An objective is a sentence you can assemble from parts. The parts below are colored consistently through the rest of this tour.

By the end of this unit, students will recommend one of two job candidates for an open position by comparing their resumes against the job posting's requirements, citing at least two specific requirements to justify the choice.

Quick check

Which word names the action the student performs — the one a stranger could watch them do and check off as done?

"Recommend." That's the verb — the action a stranger could watch happen. "One of two job candidates" is the goal (what the verb acts on). "By comparing their resumes against the job posting's requirements" is the separate method (how it gets done). Two different jobs, easy to blur together — that's exactly why they get their own colors.

Action — do what?

An observable verb. Someone could film it. Identify, compare, calculate, design, defend.

Goal — on what?

The object the verb acts on. Concrete enough to hand over. Not "concepts."

Method — by doing what?

The means, tool, or framework they must use. This is what makes it your course.

Criterion — how well?

The standard or condition. Write it once here and it becomes your rubric.

Broken — and why

"Students will understand how to make good decisions."

Verb is invisible (nobody can watch "understand") · no object you could collect · no method, so any course could claim it · no criterion, so you'll grade on vibes.

03

Assessment

The proof — the occasion where the student performs the verb and you collect evidence.

An assessment is not "a test." It's whatever makes the objective's verb visible. The verb picks the format — never the other way round. Not every assessment is graded, either: a quick formative check tells you and the student whether they're on track before the graded moment arrives.

The objective's verb is the action a student has to perform. The assessment is just whichever format lets you see them perform it — the verb stays fixed, the format doesn't have to.

  • Written memo. Recommend one of two candidates for the role in a short memo, citing at least two requirements from the job posting.
  • Recorded video. Record a two-minute video defending the pick to a skeptical hiring manager, citing specific requirements from the posting.
  • Peer debate. In pairs, argue for opposite candidates, then jointly write a one-paragraph recommendation citing the posting's requirements.

All three are equally aligned — same verb, same standard. Pick whichever format fits your class time and tools.

Quick check

Could a student pass this task by only describing both candidates, without ever picking one?

No — and that's the whole test. If a description-only answer could pass, the task has quietly dropped the objective's verb. Whatever format you choose, it has to force a choice and a justification, not just a summary of both candidates.

Not every assessment looks like the graded memo above. Assessments split into two families, and most units need both.

Formative — while it's still fixable

  • Happens during learning, not after it
  • Usually ungraded or very low-stakes
  • Purpose: catch a misconception while it's cheap to fix
  • Examples: a one-minute reflection write, a quick poll, a low-stakes group discussion

Summative — the final tally

  • Happens after learning, to sum it up
  • Almost always graded, often heavily weighted
  • Purpose: certify how well the objective was met
  • Examples: the graded memo, a final exam, a final project

The "check readiness" step in band 04 is a formative check by another name — same question, "are we on track?," asked before the summative moment instead of after it.

  • Identify / define → quiz, labeling
  • Explain → short write-up
  • Apply / calculate → problem set, lab
  • Analyze / compare → annotated comparison
  • Evaluate / recommend → memo, debate, review
  • Design / produce → artifact, portfolio

Misaligned — invisible until grading

Multiple-choice quiz on the job posting's listed requirements.

Measures recall while the objective promised a recommendation. A student who can list every requirement from memory may still be unable to choose between two candidates — and you'd never find out.

03 · Assessment — now write yours

Your turn

Imagine a student who aces this assessment but hasn't achieved your outcome. What did they do? If you can picture that student easily, the assessment measures the wrong thing.

Seam test 3

Can you actually measure the action in your objective with this assessment?

Then the assessment is measuring something else — recall instead of the verb you wrote, effort instead of the actual judgment. Change the format, not the verb.

04

Learning activity

The rehearsal space — but rehearsal is only one of three jobs this band has to do.

Activities do three different jobs: build the knowledge the verb needs, rehearse the verb itself, and let students check their own readiness. Skip one and the assessment finds the gap for you.

BuildGive them what they need before you ask them to use it.
→
RehearseModel it, try it with help, then try it alone — for free.
→
CheckA short self-test before the graded attempt, not just after.

These three jobs mirror Fink's three components of active learning — Information & Ideas, Experience, and Reflective Dialogue (Fink, 2003). The richest activities do more than one at once: a good case discussion can deliver information and give experience in the same sitting.

Before students can recommend a candidate by comparing resumes against a job posting, they need to know how to read a job posting for what actually matters — a short reading, a mini-lecture, a worked example. Plain content delivery, done in service of the objective's method.

Fink (2003) calls this "Information and Ideas." Serves the method — you can't rehearse a move nobody taught them.

1 · See it done

Instructor compares two sample resumes against a posting out loud, thinking through which requirements matter most.

Students can't copy a move they've never seen.

2 · Try it with help

Pairs compare a short resume pair against a posting with a checklist, then swap and challenge each other's pick.

A scaffold that comes off later.

3 · Be wrong for free

A short draft recommendation memo in the exact assessment format. Feedback only. No grade.

Removes most "the exam wasn't like the class" complaints.

Fink (2003) calls this "Experience" — direct, hands-on practice beats reading about the skill, whenever it's practical to arrange.

Watch for teaching to the test

If every rehearsal is an identical clone of the graded pair, students get good at that pair, not the underlying skill. Vary at least one rehearsal — a different resume pair, the same requirements — to protect transfer. And spread the three stages across the unit instead of cramming them the week before; spaced practice beats one late rehearsal.

Quick check

Which of these three stages is missing from your course right now, if any? (This one's about your course, not a fixed answer — worth sitting with.)

Before the graded attempt, give students a short self-check — a checklist of the posting's requirements, one question ("which requirement is your recommendation weakest on?"), or two minutes of paired discussion comparing picks before submitting.

Fink (2003) calls this "Reflective Dialogue" — reflection works alone (a journal entry) or with others (a quick partner check), and either counts. Serves their own judgment — they submit knowing where they stand, not hoping.

Bloom's taxonomy

Anderson & Krathwohl's revised Bloom's taxonomy (2001) orders cognitive verbs from low-lift recall to high-lift creation. Objectives near the bottom are easiest to write — and easiest to over-rely on. Objectives near the top ask more of the student, and more of the assessment that has to catch them.
